## The Problem I Couldn't Ignore

It started with a news story. An ambulance stuck in Lucknow traffic for 40 minutes while a patient inside didn't make it. I'd seen this happen in real life too — emergency vehicles trapped behind red lights while the signal blindly counted down its timer, indifferent to who was waiting.

I kept thinking: the technology to fix this already exists. Cameras are everywhere. Object detection models are open source. Why is no one connecting the dots at the intersection level?

That became Project NETRA.

## What I Set Out to Build

The goal was straightforward on paper: a system that watches a traffic camera feed, detects vehicles in real-time, and — if it spots an ambulance or fire truck — overrides the normal signal logic to clear the lane immediately.

No cloud dependency. No expensive hardware. Just a camera, a model, and a decision engine.

## Choosing YOLOv8

I evaluated a few options — older YOLO versions, SSD, even a basic OpenCV background subtraction approach. YOLOv8 by Ultralytics won for three reasons:

**Speed.** Running on Google Colab's T4 GPU, YOLOv8n processes frames at ~45 FPS. Fast enough for real intersections.

**Accuracy on small objects.** Emergency vehicles at a distance are small in frame. YOLOv8's anchor-free detection head handles this significantly better than v5.

**Fine-tuning ease.** The API is remarkably clean:

```python
from ultralytics import YOLO
model = YOLO("yolov8n.pt")
model.train(data="traffic.yaml", epochs=50, imgsz=640)
```

## The Hard Part: Data

Pre-trained YOLO knows what an ambulance looks like in Western countries. Indian ambulances look completely different — green and white, different proportions, different markings.

I spent two weeks collecting and annotating images:
- Scraped Google Images and YouTube frames for Indian emergency vehicles
- Used Roboflow to annotate bounding boxes and export in YOLO format
- Applied augmentations to increase dataset diversity

Final dataset: ~2,400 images across 4 classes — car, bus, motorcycle, ambulance.

## The Signal Logic

Detection alone is not enough. The interesting engineering is the decision layer:

```python
def process_frame(frame, model, signal_state):
    results = model(frame)[0]
    for box in results.boxes:
        cls = int(box.cls[0])
        conf = float(box.conf[0])
        if cls == AMBULANCE_CLASS and conf > 0.75:
            signal_state.trigger_priority_override(
                lane=detect_lane(box)
            )
            return signal_state
    vehicle_count = len(results.boxes)
    signal_state.update_timer(vehicle_count)
    return signal_state
```

## What I Learned

**Confidence thresholds matter enormously.** At 0.5 confidence, the model falsely flagged white SUVs as ambulances 12% of the time. At 0.75, false positives dropped under 2%.

**The camera angle is everything.** A top-down camera gives the best lane detection. Real deployment needs standardized camera positioning.

**Colab has limits.** I ended up processing video in chunks and stitching results.

## What Is Next

The current version runs on recorded video. The next step is a WebSocket-based system ingesting a live RTSP stream and pushing signal commands to a microcontroller.
